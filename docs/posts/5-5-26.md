# Eighteen 2026 RFS Posts. One Missing Substrate.

Last Updated: 2026-05-07
Version: v0.2.0

*Or: what every agentic AI thesis published this year is quietly assuming exists.*

*Danny Barbour · April 2026 · [github.com/g8e-ai/g8e](https://github.com/g8e-ai/g8e)*

---

YC published two batches of "we want to fund X" theses this year. a16z published Big Ideas 2026 across five practices. Lux is funding "compound AI systems" and "scaffolding for agents that break execution when the agent isn't sure." Read all of it back to back and a pattern emerges.

Every one of these theses assumes a substrate that nobody is building.

The substrate is the layer that sits between the AI agent and the production environment it's about to change. It's the thing that catches the agent's contextually-wrong-but-internally-consistent plan before it lands on a real system. It's the thing that enforces hard technical boundaries at the wire-protocol level, rather than relying on LLM prompt obedience. It's the thing that gives the human owner a defensible audit trail when something does land. It's the thing that lets the agent be replaced without losing history, because the audit lives on the host, not in the cloud where the model runs.

I've been heads-down building this substrate for the last year. It's called [g8e](https://github.com/g8e-ai/g8e), it's open source, and the thesis is in the [position paper](https://g8e.ai). What follows is the eighteen places I see it needed in the 2026 wishlist.

---

## What the substrate has to do

Five properties, in increasing order of how much they matter:

1. **Replaceable reasoning, irreplaceable record.** The model layer is a commodity that flips quarterly. The audit layer is the system of record for the next decade. Decouple them.
2. **Sovereign per-host execution.** Outbound-only mTLS, no inbound ports, audit ledger lives on the managed host. The cloud can disappear and history doesn't.
3. **Protocol-level enforcement.** A fail-closed, Protobuf-first contract (`UniversalEnvelope`) that binds typed payloads to L1/L2/L3 governance metadata. It strictly enforces hard L1 technical bedrock (like forbidden patterns) via reflection and validates BFT state-roots before execution can even dispatch. No backwards compatibility; legacy payloads are rejected, not migrated.
4. **Byzantine consensus over the AI layer (L2).** A single model verifying its own output is a bug. Multiple blind validators with a calibrated adversary catch what a single chain-of-thought doesn't.
5. **FIDO2 co-validation on every state change (L3).** The human's signature is the only stake the system can't fake. Every other check exists to spend that stake well. Auto-approval is authorization state only—it never bypasses L1 bedrock or L2 consensus.

That's g8e. Now here's where it lands.

## The eighteen

### 1. Software for Agents — Aaron Epstein, YC

> "The next trillion users on the internet won't be people, they'll be AI agents."

The whole RFS describes the API/MCP/CLI layer agents need. g8e is the layer one level below that: the governance plane that lets an agent actually change state in production without the human owner losing sovereignty over what happened. If you're building agent-first software, "what happens when the agent does the wrong thing" is currently hand-wavy. It needs to stop being hand-wavy through strict protocol-level enforcement and fail-closed architecture.

### 2. The AI Operating System for Companies — Diana Hu, YC

> "Made their entire company queryable… open loop into a closed loop."

Diana's RFS describes the read side. The write side is the harder problem and it's missing. A closed loop requires that the act of changing the system is itself observable, attributable, and reversible — Merkle-committed, FIDO2-signed, replayable. The intelligence layer she describes can't be load-bearing for ops without that.

### 3. Company Brain — Tom Blomfield, YC

> "An executable skills file for AI… so AI systems can use that skills file to actually do the work safely and consistently."

"Safely and consistently" is doing all the work in that sentence. The Tribunal plus co-validation is the mechanism that converts a skills file from "search index over Slack" into "thing that can take action." Without the substrate, the company brain is a search engine. With it, it's an operating system where safety is bound directly to the operator wire protocol.

### 4. Inference Chips for Agent Workflows — Diana Hu, YC

> "Models loop: calling tools, branching, backtracking, holding context across dozens of steps."

The agent loop is the unit of work. g8e is the orchestration layer that proves the loop terminated correctly. Anyone shipping silicon for agent loops needs reference deployments running real loops in production with verifiable correctness. That's a partnership conversation, not a vendor pitch.

### 5. AI-Native Service Companies — Gustaf Alströmer, YC

> "They don't sell software — they sell the service."

If you're selling the service — insurance brokerage, accounting, audit, compliance — you're now liable for the agent's actions. g8e converts "we hope the agent did the right thing" into "we have a cryptographic audit trail of every state change with human co-signatures, signed protocol envelopes, and a 90-day forensic replay." That's the difference between an SLA you can sign and one your insurer won't let you.

### 6. AI for Government — Tom Blomfield, YC

> "Selling to government is extremely hard, but once you've figured out how to land your first customer, they tend to be very sticky."

g8e was almost designed for this market: Apache 2.0, self-hosted, no telemetry, air-gap capable, NSA Zero Trust alignment, FIDO2 only, no SaaS dependency. Every procurement objection to AI in government is a governance objection. The protocol-level guarantees dissolve them.

### 7. American Dynamism — Erin Price-Wright, Katherine Boyle, a16z

> "Defense, public safety, education, supply chain, industrials, manufacturing… companies that view the government as a customer."

Dual-use AI in defense and public safety is the highest-stakes deployment of agentic systems on earth. The audit ledger, Byzantine consensus, strict envelope state-root verification, and "no inbound ports on managed hosts" architecture aren't features here — they're a precondition for procurement. As a Veteran-Owned Small Business with a TS-eligible founder, this map is more personal than commercial.

### 8. Counter-Swarm Defense — Tyler Bosmeny, YC

> "Drone defense is looking less and less like operating a weapon and more like running a real-time distributed system. The winning companies will look more like Cloudflare than Raytheon."

This is a literal description of g8e's architecture: outbound mTLS, sovereign edge operators, strict Protobuf-first envelopes that reject malformed commands, Byzantine consensus, no central kill switch. If counter-swarm is a distributed-systems problem, the governance plane for that distributed system is the bottleneck — and right now, it doesn't exist.

### 9. AI Personalized Medicine — Ankit Gupta, YC

> "n of 1 genetic therapies… FDA has expressed more openness."

n=1 means every action is irreversible and the patient is the only person who can validate intent. Co-validation with a human signature on every state change is the only architecture the FDA can defend in front of Congress. The scrubbed vault keeps PHI on-host while the AI layer reasons on metadata — HIPAA-aligned by construction, not by promise.

### 10. AI-Native Hedge Funds — Charlie Holtz, YC

> "Swarms of agents combing through 10-Ks, earnings calls… and making trades."

The trade-authorization boundary is where this entire RFS lives or dies. SEC-defensible audit, deterministic replay of agent reasoning, Byzantine consensus on candidate trades, human signature on execution, and state-root verification to prevent execution on stale data. That's not features — it's the legal foundation a fund's compliance officer needs in order to sign off on any of it.

### 11. Infra for Government Fraud Hunters — Garry Tan, YC

> "Take an insider tip and organize the evidence around it… package the findings into complaint-ready files."

Chain of custody is the entire game. A git-backed Merkle ledger plus FIDO2-signed actions tied to protocol envelopes produces evidence packages that survive cross-examination. Whistleblower law firms care about exactly this construct and nobody is building it for them.

### 12. AI-Native Discovery Engines — Jon Xu, YC

> "Design-make-test-analyze loop: models propose candidate molecules, automated labs synthesize and test them."

When the "make" step costs real money or generates real chemistry, the human-in-the-loop signature on synthesis orders is non-negotiable. The Tribunal-Auditor-human flow is a closed scientific loop with reproducibility built into the audit ledger and verified by protocol-level HMAC signatures. The discovery substrate that doesn't poison itself with hallucinated experiments.

### 13. AI-Native Agencies — Aaron Epstein, YC

> "An ad agency that uses AI to create stunning video ads… A law firm that uses AI to write legal docs in minutes."

When an agency takes action on a client's accounts — ad spend, filings, deployments — the client needs proof of what the agency's agent did. g8e is the substrate that lets agencies offer "AI-native and auditable" as a single value prop instead of a contradiction.

### 14. SaaS Challengers — Jared Friedman, YC

> "Go after the products that seem invulnerable: chip design software, ERPs, industrial control systems."

ERPs and ICS run state changes that cost millions if wrong. Anyone replacing them with AI-native software needs governance built in from day one, not bolted on at SOC 2 audit time. The substrate is inheritable trust; challengers can ship with hard L1 technical gates and L2 consensus built into the wire protocol instead of building one in year three.

### 15. Cursor for Product Managers — Andrew Miklas, YC

> "Get the outline of a new feature complete with an explanation based on customer feedback."

g8e's Sage agent already does this internally — articulates intent into a structured document before downstream agents touch anything. The "intent doc as artifact" pattern is the architecture, and it's exportable.

### 16. Startups That Want to Sell to Huge Companies — Harshita Arora & Brad Flora, YC

> "F100 size companies… looking for teams that can bend AI to solve key problems for them."

F100 procurement teams reject AI vendors on three things: data exfiltration, audit gaps, and lock-in. g8e clears all three by construction — scrubbed-only egress, on-host audit, model-agnostic, and strict fail-closed protocol enforcement. Selling g8e-powered tooling into F100 is a shorter procurement cycle than selling SaaS.

### 17. Compound AI Systems — Grace Isford, Lux Capital

> "Scaffolding a compound AI system clearly delineating how all existing underlying frameworks, tools and humans work together… agents that break execution when the agent isn't sure."

This is g8e in plain English. Lux's portfolio (Cognition, Applied Compute, Together) lives on top of a substrate that "breaks execution when the agent isn't sure." Variance and Nemesis are the agents that *are* unsure on purpose, and the Auditor is the layer that hears them out.

### 18. AI Guidance for Physical Work — David Lieb, YC

> "Real-time AI guidance… 'turn off that valve', 'use the ⅜ inch wrench'."

Field-service AI that gives instructions to humans operating physical equipment is a co-validation problem in disguise. The AI proposes; the human signs; the audit ledger records. Insurance and OSHA exposure for these companies depends on having a defensible record of every guidance event — which, like everything else on this list, doesn't exist yet.

## Why none of this is built

Three reasons.

**One.** Most of the people building agent infrastructure in 2026 came up after the era when production infrastructure had to be defensible in court. They've never been on the bridge of a Navy vessel watching a system fail and known they were the person responsible. They've never been the on-call engineer for a global backup environment that actually has to restore. The instinct to design for adversarial conditions — including adversarial conditions inside your own model — has to be earned somewhere, and the model providers haven't earned it because they've never been the ones liable.

**Two.** "Governance" got branded as a slow-moving compliance category and the fast-moving agent builders consequently can't see that what they're missing *is* governance, just architected for the speed they actually move at. The Byzantine-consensus reasoning layer plus on-host audit plus FIDO2 co-validation runs in milliseconds. It's not slow. It's just not what shipped first, and what shipped first isn't governance — it's an approval modal.

**Three.** Building the substrate requires being able to write a 4MB statically-linked Go binary, design a Byzantine-consensus protocol over LLM personas, implement FIDO2 properly, bind hard technical gates directly to a Protobuf wire contract, and own the threat model end to end. That's not a stack any single team has on the org chart. The agent companies have AI engineers. The infrastructure companies have systems engineers. Almost nobody has both at the seniority where they can hold the whole architecture in their head.
